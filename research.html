An Immunology Laboratory Focusing on B Cells

One of the central questions in immunology is the identification of "self" and "not-self". As an important part of adaptive immunity, B cells continue to evolve by forming a huge antibody library and, with the help of germinal center T cells, stimulating unique somatic hypermutation and producing specific antibodies for recognition and elimination. pathogenic microorganisms; on the other hand, the body maintains tolerance to itself through negative selection of B cells that bind to self-antigens. Through the immune memory formed by long-lasting antibodies and memory B cells, the body can quickly recognize the re-invasion of pathogenic microorganisms.

In infectious diseases or vaccination, how does the body form long-lasting and broad-spectrum antibodies to respond to evolving pathogens? How are pathogenic autoantibodies formed in autoimmune diseases? Whether and how modifiable environmental factors (such as commensal microorganisms) influence B cell differentiation and antibody production?

Focusing on these issues, our laboratory focuses on B cell-mediated immunology, based on mouse models and human longitudinal cohorts, combining molecular biology, biochemistry, in vivo imaging, cell lineage tracing and bioinformatics, to study the following 4 Of particular interest in research on:
